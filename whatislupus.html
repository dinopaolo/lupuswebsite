What is Lupus

Lupus is a chronic autoimmune disease in which the body's immune system becomes hyperactive and attacks normal, healthy tissue. This results in symptoms such as inflammation, swelling, and damage to joints, skin, kidneys, blood, the heart, and lungs.

Lupus makes the immune system unable to differentiate between antigens (a substance capable of inducing a specific immune response) and healthy tissue. This leads the immune system to direct antibodies against the healthy tissue - not just antigens - causing swelling, pain, and tissue damage.

Types of lupus

The 3 main types of lupus are

Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - the most common form of lupus. The word 'systemic' means that he disease can involve many parts of the body (internal). It can affect a personâ€™s quality of life through pain, fatigue, and associated depression and anxiety.

Discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE) - typically a milder type of lupus that usually affects the skin. Symptoms include scaly marks on the skin, hair loss or thinning and bald patches. People affected by DLE may have to avoid direct sunlight.

Drug-induced lupus (DILE) - similar to SLE, symptoms are usually milder. DILE may be induced by medications or caused by other compounds in the environment.
